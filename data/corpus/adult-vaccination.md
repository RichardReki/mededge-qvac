# Adult Vaccination

## Overview
Vaccines help the immune system recognize and fight specific infections, protecting both the individual and the wider community. While many vaccines are given in childhood, adults also benefit from booster doses and additional vaccines based on age, health conditions, occupation, and travel. Staying up to date helps prevent serious illness and complications.

## Common symptoms
- Vaccines are preventive, so they are given before illness rather than to treat symptoms
- Mild, short-lived reactions are common, such as a sore arm, mild fever, or tiredness
- These reactions usually settle within a day or two and reflect the immune response
- Serious reactions are rare but should be reported promptly

## Common causes / risk factors
- Older age, which increases vulnerability to certain infections
- Chronic conditions such as heart disease, lung disease, or diabetes
- A weakened immune system or pregnancy
- Healthcare or other occupational exposures
- International travel to areas with specific disease risks
- Incomplete or outdated childhood vaccination history

## Typical management
- Keep a personal record of vaccinations and review it regularly
- Common adult vaccines include annual influenza, COVID-19 (per current guidance), and tetanus-diphtheria-pertussis boosters
- Age- or risk-based vaccines may include pneumococcal, shingles, and hepatitis vaccines
- Discuss travel vaccines well before departure
- Follow national immunization schedules and professional advice
- People with specific conditions should confirm which vaccines are recommended or should be avoided

## When to seek urgent care
- Difficulty breathing, or swelling of the face, lips, or throat after a vaccine
- Widespread hives, dizziness, or fainting shortly after vaccination
- A very high fever that does not settle
- Severe or persistent symptoms that worry you
- Any rapid, unusual reaction following immunization
