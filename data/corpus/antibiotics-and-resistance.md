# Antibiotics and Antibiotic Resistance

## Overview
Antibiotics are medicines that treat infections caused by bacteria, not viruses. Antibiotic resistance happens when bacteria change so that the medicines designed to kill them no longer work, making infections harder to treat. Resistance is driven largely by overuse and misuse of antibiotics, and it is a growing global health concern.

## Common symptoms
- Antibiotics treat bacterial infections; they do not help colds, flu, or most coughs and sore throats, which are usually viral
- Signs that may suggest a bacterial infection include persistent high fever, worsening localized pain, or pus
- A resistant infection may not improve despite taking antibiotics as prescribed
- Possible side effects of antibiotics include nausea, diarrhea, and allergic reactions

## Common causes / risk factors
- Taking antibiotics when they are not needed (such as for viral illnesses)
- Not finishing a prescribed course as directed
- Using leftover or someone else's antibiotics
- Frequent or unnecessary antibiotic use
- Overuse of antibiotics in agriculture and livestock
- Poor infection prevention and hygiene

## Typical management
- Use antibiotics only when prescribed by a qualified professional
- Take the right antibiotic, at the right dose, for the full recommended course
- Do not pressure clinicians for antibiotics when they are not indicated
- Never share or save antibiotics for later use
- Prevent infections through hand hygiene, safe food handling, and recommended vaccinations
- Narrow-spectrum antibiotics are often preferred when an appropriate option is available

## When to seek urgent care
- Signs of a severe infection: high fever, confusion, or a rapid heartbeat
- Difficulty breathing, swelling of the face, lips, or throat (possible allergic reaction)
- A widespread rash, hives, or fainting after taking an antibiotic
- Severe or bloody diarrhea during or after a course of antibiotics
- An infection that rapidly spreads, with redness, swelling, and intense pain
