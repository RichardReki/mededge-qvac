# GERD (Acid Reflux)

## Overview
Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD) is a condition in which stomach acid frequently flows back up into the esophagus, irritating its lining. Occasional reflux is common, but GERD refers to persistent symptoms that occur regularly and may affect quality of life. It results from a weakened or relaxed valve (the lower esophageal sphincter) between the stomach and esophagus.

## Common symptoms
- Heartburn, a burning feeling in the chest, often after eating
- Regurgitation of sour or bitter fluid into the throat or mouth
- Difficulty or discomfort when swallowing
- A persistent cough or hoarse voice
- Sensation of a lump in the throat
- Symptoms worse when lying down or bending over

## Common causes / risk factors
- Excess body weight or obesity
- Large or fatty meals and eating close to bedtime
- Trigger foods such as spicy or acidic foods, chocolate, caffeine, and alcohol
- Smoking
- Pregnancy
- Hiatal hernia

## Typical management
- Eat smaller meals and avoid lying down for two to three hours afterward
- Identify and reduce trigger foods and drinks
- Lose excess weight and stop smoking
- Raise the head of the bed for nighttime symptoms
- Medication classes: antacids for quick relief, H2 receptor blockers, and proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) to reduce acid production
- Persistent or severe cases may need endoscopy or specialist review

## When to seek urgent care
- Difficulty or pain when swallowing, or food getting stuck
- Vomiting blood or passing black, tarry stools
- Unintended weight loss
- Chest pain that could be cardiac, especially with breathlessness or sweating
- Choking or persistent symptoms despite treatment
