# Iron-Deficiency Anemia

## Overview
Iron-deficiency anemia occurs when the body does not have enough iron to make adequate hemoglobin, the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen. As a result, tissues receive less oxygen, causing tiredness and other symptoms. It is the most common type of anemia and is often identified through blood tests showing low hemoglobin and low ferritin (a marker of iron stores).

## Common symptoms
- Fatigue and weakness
- Pale skin
- Shortness of breath on exertion
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Cold hands and feet
- Brittle nails and hair loss
- Unusual cravings for non-food items such as ice (pica)

## Common causes / risk factors
- Blood loss, including heavy menstrual periods
- Gastrointestinal bleeding (for example, ulcers or polyps)
- Diet low in iron-rich foods
- Poor iron absorption (such as in celiac disease)
- Pregnancy, which increases iron demand
- Frequent blood donation

## Typical management
- Identify and treat the underlying cause of iron loss
- Eat iron-rich foods such as lean red meat, beans, lentils, and leafy greens
- Pair plant-based iron with vitamin C to improve absorption
- Oral iron supplements (iron salts) are commonly used to rebuild stores
- Intravenous iron or other treatment when oral iron is not tolerated or effective
- Recheck blood counts and ferritin to confirm improvement

## When to seek urgent care
- Chest pain, rapid heartbeat, or severe shortness of breath
- Fainting or severe dizziness
- Vomiting blood or passing black, tarry, or bloody stools
- Very heavy or uncontrolled bleeding
- Extreme weakness or confusion
