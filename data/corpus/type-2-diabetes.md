# Type 2 Diabetes

## Overview
Type 2 diabetes is a long-term condition in which the body becomes resistant to insulin or does not produce enough of it, leading to elevated blood glucose (sugar) levels. Over time, high blood sugar can damage blood vessels, nerves, kidneys, and eyes. It is commonly monitored using HbA1c, a marker that reflects average blood glucose over the past two to three months.

## Common symptoms
- Increased thirst and frequent urination
- Tiredness and low energy
- Unexplained weight loss
- Blurred vision
- Slow-healing cuts or frequent infections
- Tingling or numbness in the hands or feet

## Common causes / risk factors
- Excess body weight, especially around the abdomen
- Physical inactivity and a diet high in refined sugars and processed foods
- Family history of diabetes
- Older age
- History of gestational diabetes
- Certain ethnic backgrounds with higher baseline risk

## Typical management
- Adopt a balanced diet emphasizing vegetables, whole grains, lean protein, and reduced refined carbohydrates
- Engage in regular physical activity and aim for gradual weight loss if overweight
- Monitor blood glucose and HbA1c levels as advised
- Common first-line medication class is metformin (a biguanide)
- Additional classes when needed: SGLT2 inhibitors, GLP-1 receptor agonists, DPP-4 inhibitors, sulfonylureas, and insulin
- Manage blood pressure, cholesterol, and foot and eye health to prevent complications

## When to seek urgent care
- Very high blood sugar with confusion, drowsiness, or fruity-smelling breath
- Signs of dangerously low blood sugar: shaking, sweating, confusion, or loss of consciousness
- Persistent vomiting and inability to keep fluids down
- Chest pain or shortness of breath
- A foot wound that is infected, blackened, or not healing
