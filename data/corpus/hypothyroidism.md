# Hypothyroidism

## Overview
Hypothyroidism is a condition in which the thyroid gland does not produce enough thyroid hormone, slowing down many of the body's processes. It is commonly detected through blood tests showing a raised thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) alongside low or low-normal thyroid hormone (free T4). Symptoms often develop gradually and can be easy to overlook.

## Common symptoms
- Persistent tiredness and low energy
- Weight gain despite no change in diet
- Feeling cold more easily than usual
- Dry skin and hair, and hair thinning
- Constipation
- Low mood, sluggish thinking, or poor concentration
- Muscle aches and slowed heart rate

## Common causes / risk factors
- Autoimmune thyroid disease (such as Hashimoto's thyroiditis)
- Previous thyroid surgery or radioactive iodine treatment
- Certain medications affecting the thyroid
- Iodine deficiency or excess
- Family history of thyroid disease
- More common in women and with increasing age

## Typical management
- Confirm the diagnosis with TSH and free T4 blood tests
- Replace the missing hormone with levothyroxine (a synthetic thyroid hormone)
- Take the medication consistently, usually on an empty stomach
- Recheck thyroid blood tests periodically to adjust the dose
- Maintain a balanced diet and report new or worsening symptoms
- Lifelong treatment is usually required, with regular monitoring

## When to seek urgent care
- Severe drowsiness, confusion, or unresponsiveness
- Very low body temperature, especially in older adults
- Slow breathing or markedly slowed heart rate
- Chest pain or significant shortness of breath
- A noticeably swollen face or difficulty swallowing or breathing
